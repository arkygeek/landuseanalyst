The values used in Table E.1 have been derived from a variety of sources. It can
rightfully be argued that any one of these values might have been higher or lower; for
example there is no possible way to know the exact yield of any crop in the past, so how
can a yield of 500 kg/ha be justified? Even today, farmers do not know what the yield
of any particular field will be until they harvest it, and that yield can be very different
even from another field on ‘the other side of the fence’. The values used are, for the
purposes of the model, approximations that, even if not perfect, will not invalidate the
outcome of the model given the imprecise nature of many of the other variables which
are used (e.g. variables where minimum and maximum values have been adopted,
such as population levels, will ‘absorb’ imprecisions regarding the input factors used
to define crops). Additionally, there are other major influencing factors that cannot be
known such as sowing rates, harvesting efficiency, and so on.
In the interests of consistency for use in the model, and to compensate for loss
of energy due to cooking and other factors (as well as to simplify the model), I have
opted to use conservative and ‘even’ values which have been based on a large number
of sources ranging from personal communications and animal science websites to aca593

any one source in their entirety, but they are reasonable approximations. Some of the

been based upon USDA figures, which, for example, state that wheat has approximately
3.2 to 3.4 Mcals per kg and that pulses contain 3.2 to 3.5 Mcals (USDA 2009). Straw
and chaff (fodder) values adapted from Simms and Russell (1997, Table I).
