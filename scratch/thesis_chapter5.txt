6.1 to 11.4 ha of land for every adult member of the herd.
The contribution of dairy to the overall diet for Scenarios 21, 22, 23, 24 is 9%, 9%,
18%, 18% leaving plants responsible for supplying 71%, 71%, 62%, 62% of the overall
diet.
Scenarios 19-24 (Figure 5.86) are showing a complete utilisation of the NE corner
of the Jordan valley, stretching to both the Jordan and Yarmouk rivers. In all of these
scenarios the hills to the east are being utilised to a fairly large degree for both crop
and grazing land. There is a large difference in the amount of crop land between the
scenarios when the animals are being foddered and those when they are not. They is
a very large amount of grazing land completely surrounding the site, extending well
across both rivers and high into the hills. There appears to be a fairly equal amount
of grazing land in the hills compared to the grazing land in the valley. There is a very
wide band of grazing land to the south of the crop catchment in the valley as well as
being interspersed with the orchards on the slopes to the east. So it is therefore now
quite possible that the land across the rivers would have been used for grazing as there
would be little room for expansion either to the south or to the east.
There is undoubtedly a very significant impact on the landscape in the valley. The
extent of the catchment area is large, extending up to 8 km from the settlement. The
substantial amount of grazing is visibly evident, even in scenarios where the animals
are being foddered. When animals are foddered, dairy utilisation has negligible effect
on both the footprint and the crop land to grazing land ratio. When the animals are
not foddered however, a change in crop land to grazing land ratio is visible, but not to
extent where it would cause any appreciable differences in the effect on the landscape.
As suggested in discussions for Set 3 (Figure 5.79, page 224), these scenarios likely
reflect the actual contribution of meat in the diet; meat is 20% (the mid-range value)
and, although dairy utilisation is now affecting landuse, it is not affecting the cropland
to grazing land ratio to such an extent that it makes a drastic difference. This suggests
that these scenarios would be an acceptable scenario for a maximum average extent.
To this point, Sets 3 and 4 seem to be the best scenarios as representative of landuse at
Shuna in the EEBI, with the best overall estimate somewhere between the two.

Set 5 (Figure 5.93) considers the effect of animal foddering for a minimum population of 1000 people, for a 30% meat level and dairy levels of 0%, 50% and 100%.
Scenarios 25, 27 and 29 are assuming the animals were foddered; Scenarios 26, 28 and
30 are assuming the animals were not foddered. Minimal variance in footprint size is
evident. There is a visible disparity in the crop land to grazing land ratio. The grazing
land area needed increases from [3157, 3203, 3248] ha to [3918, 3964, 4009] ha when animals are not given supplemental feed. The increase of 123.43% (values from Figures

2481 ha. Thus, if the adult cattle were being utilised for traction, there would be from
2.8 to 8.1 ha of land for every adult member of the herd.
The contribution of dairy to the overall diet for Scenarios 27, 28, 29, 30 is 13%,
13%, 27%, 27% leaving plants responsible for supplying 57%, 57%, 43%, 43% of the
overall diet.
The 30% meat level scenarios can not be discounted in the EEBI, as there are, as
previously discussed, indications that this period did have quite a ‘meaty’ diet. The effects of dairy utilisation are quite profound in the scenarios where animals are foddered
. In fact, not foddering the animals will greatly reduce any impact on the landscape
caused by the effects of cultivation. For example, looking at Figure 5.93f, where the
meat level is at 30% and dairy utilisation is 100%, the area of crop land is only 862
Ha. We see areas greater than this in the Chalcolithic (eg. the entirety of Group 3,
Figure 5.57 on page 196). Therefore even with these extreme circumstances, for a
minimum population these would be very sustainable scenarios as they would have
minimal effect on the landscape.
Scenarios 25-30 (Figure 5.93) require most of the grazing land to be situated in
the valley. There is some grazing land in the hills and, in the foddered scenarios (Figures 5.93a to 5.93c) there is crop land extending into the hills to the east which is likely
where olive orchards and viticulture would have been.
When the animals are not foddered (Figures 5.93d to 5.93f) there is less crop land
on the slopes to the east so the overall footprint of the crop land areas may have altered
slightly in order to provide more crop land to be situated on the slopes to accommodate
the olive and vine crops. The Wadi would have provided areas for the pigs which were
clearly important to EEBI Shuna. These observations reinforce the sustainable nature
of these scenarios, indicating that a 30% meat level in EEBI Shuna with a population
of 1000 was viable.

36, Figure 5.104i), there is not a significant amount of change in the crop catchment
area for the remaining five scenarios. In these five scenarios, even Scenario 34 (Figure 5.104h, page 257) which contains the smallest amount of crop land (3371 Ha), the
settlement still would have had a large enough area of cropland to have had made a
significant impact on the landscape. The cropland stretches from the settlement to the
Jordan river, high into the hills to the east, and almost all the way north to the Yarmouk
river. As discussed previously, the massive amounts of grazing land required to maintain the settlement in the high meat scenarios when animals were not being foddered
would very likely have caused problems if any neighbouring settlements needed the
valley land for their own subsistence purposes.

5.7.3

Late EB I Results

The faunal records for Shuna in the LEBI indicate that there was a much lower portion of pig remains in the assemblage and a greatly increased portion of cattle (see
Table 5.10 on page 319). Given the large increase in cattle occurring simultaneously
with the almost complete disappearance of pigs it seems reasonable to suggest that
meat played a reduced role in the LEBI diet. Cattle are capable of producing a lot of
milk, so an increase in their numbers also elevates the potential for utilisation of dairy
products. The greater population of cattle could also be an indicator of cattle being
used as draught animals, also implying a greater production potential/efficiency for
crops. An increase in agricultural production efficiency also increases the likelihood
that the people of Shuna were foddering their animals.
Given these inferences, it is reasonable to suggest that in the LEBI, the diet is less
likely to be accurately represented with a 30% meat contribution than with the other
levels used of 10% and 20%. Likewise, scenarios where dairy utilisation is 0% are not
likely to accurately depict the LEBI situation. As it will be shown in the remainder
of this section, the catchments created using 30% meat contributions result in visibly
different landuse patterns that lend strength to the inferences noted above.

The model produces significant catchments, with crop land not only focussed in the
valley, but now starting to stretch more visibly into the hills to the east where olives
and vine crops would be most significant. The catchment is engulfing nearly the entire

land from both the Jordan and Yarmouk rivers.
Given the significant amount of land required for crop production, it is possible that
the land being used for crops would have been fairly efficiently clustered around the
site. This would have a great impact on the landscape given the large, densely used
crop area, intensifying the impact of landuse associated with cultivation. Although the
bands for grazing are relatively narrow, they still extend for a considerable distance
into the hills, giving the shepherds access to a range of ecological zones for pasturing.
Foddering or not foddering the animals results in very little difference in the area
of cultivated land, the overall footprint of the settlement, or the ratio between crop
land and grazing land. In fact, all 6 of the scenarios in Set 1 are producing very
similar catchment areas. However, they are certainly all large enough to have affected
travel through the valley on the east side of the Jordan river, with it being seemingly
impossible to do so without coming into contact with the settlement of Shuna.
For the six scenarios in Set 1, the effect of dairy utilisation is essentially nil, but due
to the suspected decreased reliance on meat in the LEBI, and likely increased utilisation
of dairy products, population level increases should amplify the effect dairy utilisation
levels have upon the landuse of the settlement.

adult, indicating a much higher density of cattle in relation to crop land area in this
period. There are 401 adult cattle, which is a substantial herd size.
Dairy production potential is high, and the 0% dairy utilisation scenarios are not
likely to be representative of LEBI Shuna. Given the near absence of pigs and thus less
‘meaty’ diet in the LEBI, all scenarios with a 10% meat contribution need to be given
high consideration as being representative of Shuna in the LEBI.

The number of adult cattle in scenarios 7-12 is 1201, as is shown in Figures 5.112

to 9359 ha. Thus, if the adult cattle were being utilised for traction, there would be

The contribution of dairy to the overall diet for Scenarios 9, 10, 11, 12 is 6%, 6%,

Scenarios 7-12 produce unsurprisingly bigger zones than those which we saw for
the minimum population in Set 1 (Figure 5.111, page 265). A dramatic increase in the
size of the herd of cattle occurs, from 401 to 1201 but the same ratio of cattle to crop
land remains at 5.7-7.8 ha per adult animal.
Dairy utilisation seemingly makes little difference to the Set 2 scenarios. Set 2
catchments (Scenarios 7-12) are suggestive of a heavy dependancy on crop production.
In comparison to the EEBI (Figure 5.72, page 215), the area of crop land has not
increased substantially. For example, the area for crop land for Scenario 2 in Set 2 in
the EEBI is 6454 ha (Figure 5.72a) and Set 2 Scenario 12 in the LEBI (Figure 5.118f)
produces a crop catchment of 6809 ha, a difference of only 355 Ha. The difference
in extent is only a three minute increase for the crop land extents; 167 minutes from
164 minutes. Impact on the landscape then is going to be very similar to the EEBI Set
2 scenarios. The area of crop land is completely encompassing the NE corner of the
valley (as defined by the Yarmouk and Jordan rivers). There is also a small area of crop
land across the Jordan river to the west and, in the scenarios where the animals are
being foddered (Figures 5.118d to 5.118f), across the Yarmouk river to the north. It
is possible that this would not have occurred. It would likely have been preferred to
simply venture further south into the valley or east into the hills, as crossing the river
each day to tend to the crop land or moving a herd of animals across a river would
have been at the very least an inconvenience.
Foddering the animals makes very little difference to the landuse pattern. The
catchment areas in all six scenarios are, as mention previously, completely encompassing the NE corner of the valley regardless of animal foddering.
Considering either minimum or maximum population estimates, the settlement
would have had a huge presence in the region; with such a large footprint and in
particular such a large area of crop land, it undoubtedly would be imprinting itself
on the landscape. It would have been nearly impossible to not have had intereactions
with people from LEBI Shuna if travelling through the valley. LEBI Shuna was large,
and under these scenarios required massive cropland catchments which would have
dominated the landscape, the large swathes and fields making powerful statements to
their neighbours.

Set 4 (Figure 5.132) considers the effect of animal foddering for a maximum population of 6000 people, for a 20% meat level and dairy levels of 0%, 50% and 100%.
Scenarios 19, 21 and 23 are assuming the animals were foddered; Scenarios 20, 22
and 24 are assuming the animals were not foddered.
Minimal variance in footprint size is evident in the LEBI when modelling the settlement using the maximum population (6000) and a meat contribution of 20%. There is
a visible disparity in the crop land to grazing land ratio. The grazing land area needed
increases from [10347, 10534, 10721] ha to [12833, 13020, 13207] ha when animals are
not given supplemental feed. The increase of 123.19% (values from Figures 5.132c

The number of adult cattle in scenarios 19-24 is 2402, as is shown in Figures 5.126

to 5.131 (pages 284 to 289). The area of cropland in these scenarios ranges from 4901
to 10001 ha. Thus, if the adult cattle were being utilised for traction, there would be

The contribution of dairy to the overall diet for Scenarios 21, 22, 23, 24 is 12%,
12%, 24%, 24% leaving plants responsible for supplying 68%, 68%, 56%, 56% of the
overall diet.
The scenarios in Set 4 (with the maximum population of 6000) show an obvious
increase in the overall footprint size of the settlement when compared to the minimum
population shown in Set 3 (Figure 5.125, page 5.125), however a similar pattern can
be seen. Again, animal foddering is having a major effect on the use of land; when the
animals are being foddered (Figures 5.132a to 5.132c) there is a much greater crop
land to grazing land ratio than when the animals are not foddered (Figures 5.132d to
5.132f), and once more, the overall footprint of the settlement does not differ a great
deal.
When animals are foddered, demands on crop land are elevated, which results in
these scenarios having cropland catchments extending beyond both rivers; in contrast,
when animals are not foddered, the cropland catchments generated barely extend beyond the Jordan river. Not foddering the animals reduces the area of crop land by
approximately one third, but this reduced area is certainly large enough to have certainly had an effect on the landscape. In Set 4 the Crop land consumes all of the land
in the NE corner of the valley and there is an additional significant amount of grazing
land required for both the foddered and not foddered scenarios which could have been
potentially problematic if this overlapped with landuse requirements of other sites.
Similarly to Set 3, dairy is having some effect on the area of crop land, but not a
dramatic one. Considering that there would almost certainly have been at least some
utilisation of dairy in the LEBI, we can exclude the two scenarios (19 and 20) which
utilise 0% dairy. Therefore dairy is not having much of an effect on landuse in these
four remaining scenarios as neither the area of crop land nor the overall footprint
changes significantly when we compare the 50% and 100% dairy levels. Similar to Set
3 this meat 20% level would be consistent with the less ‘meatier’ LEBI diet, but should
perhaps be regarded as an upper limit.
With such a large use of land being consumed by the settlement of Shuna, the settlement itself would have been clearly visible and unavoidable when travelling through
this region, especially on the east bank of the River Jordan.

and 30 are assuming the animals were not foddered.
Minimal variance in footprint size is evident in the LEBI when modelling the settlement using the minimum population (2000) and a meat contribution of 30%. There is
a visible disparity in the crop land to grazing land ratio. The grazing land area needed
increases from [5437, 5530, 5623] ha to [6680, 6774, 6867] ha when animals are not given
supplemental feed. The increase of 122.12% (values from Figures 5.139c and 5.139f

The number of adult cattle in scenarios 25-30 is 1201, as is shown in Figures 5.133

to 5.138 (pages 292 to 297). The area of cropland in these scenarios ranges from 998
to 3549 ha. Thus, if the adult cattle were being utilised for traction, there would be
from 0.8 to 2.9 ha of land for every adult member of the herd.
The contribution of dairy to the overall diet for Scenarios 27, 28, 29, 30 is 18%,
18%, 36%, 36% leaving plants responsible for supplying 52%, 52%, 34%, 34% of the
overall diet.
In Set 5, the effects of animal foddering on crop land/landuse are very apparent.
Cropland ranges from 3549 ha when the animals are being foddered (Scenario 25
(Figure 5.139a)) to only 998 ha when the animals are not being foddered (Scenario
30 (Figure 5.139f)) which is more than three times the area of crop land required to
fodder the animals.
Even for these minimum population scenarios, the amount of grazing land required
is vast, especially when the animals are not being foddered. Grazing land in these
scenarios extends just beyond the River Jordan but it is possible that they would have
chosen to use more land to the south or the east to avoid crossing the river with their
animals.
The crop footprint is actually under 1000 ha and comparing this to the EEBI scenarios shows crop footprint areas of a similar size in the maximum population of the
EEBI (1000); Group 2 of the EEBI, Scenarios 28 and 30 (Figures 5.102h and 5.102h,
page 253) cropland catchments have areas of 1121 Ha and 855 Ha respectively.
LEBI Shuna with a population level of 2000 would almost certainly have been sustainable under these scenarios. In particular, Scenarios 28 and 30 appear quite sustainable for this minimum population of 2000 people. With such small areas of crop land,
the impact on the landscape would be minimal. However, faunal records in the LEBI
imply a less ‘meaty’ diet (focus shifted from pigs in the Chalcolithic and EEBI to cattle
in the LEBI), so even though they appear sustainable, these 30% meat scenarios are
not likely representative of this period.

The intent of the analysis determines what variables become critical. For network
study, especially on a regional level, less importance might be given to the crop:grazing
land area ratio, with more attention paid to the overall footprint. This is because the
methodology proposed in this research assumes 100% utilisation of the land’s production potential, whether for sowing or grazing. In this model, contemporary, neighbouring settlements can not ‘re-use’ land for food production if another settlement has
already exploited it5.7 . If one wishes to examine in detail the impact a particular community may have had, or look for indications of them intentionally inscribing themselves onto their landscape, the ratio becomes important. The different relationships
mean different experiences for different concentrations of people.
The highest concentration of people on the land is obviously in the settlement itself
(and/or the area in its immediate vicinity), and it would be here that the impact from
the site would be greatest. The land here would have become highly compacted, spaces
would have been designated for specific tasks, and so forth. People here would tend to
have had a very intimate connection to ‘their’ areas, whether it be their homes or areas
where they habitually performed their everyday tasks. This intimate type of relationship could result in people having quite strong defensive feelings about this ‘zone’, even
possibly regarding areas of it as ‘their own’. With respect to their feelings towards the
settlement as a whole (meaning the space containing the buildings and it’s immediate

Another type of relationship with the landscape would have been with the agricultural land where crops were sown. Clearly, as population levels increased, so to would
the amount of food being consumed. This would have led to an increase in food production, and the easiest way to accomplish this is to either plant more crops, or raise
more animals. If the crop production was increased by sowing more land, it would have
been further away from the centre than the land already being used for this purpose.
What this leads to is an expansion of cropland from the site. Because of this, it can be
assumed that the land nearest the site would have been in ‘use’ longer than that at the
outermost extents. This is beneficial in a few ways to the people there.
The following discussion presents one possible outline of events that could have
occurred. While there are certainly many different ways that events could have unfolded, this is a scenario that is easily related to in the modern day. The purpose of
presenting this scenario is to illustrate that changes in landuse can play a role in social
development. If the population grows (by means other than immigration), the persons
already tending the fields would almost certainly have continued to do so, which can
be explained as follows. First, people would have had a strong connection to this land
after farming it repeatedly. They would have developed an intimate knowledge of the
plots of land they were working, possibly identifying themselves with it even if there
was no concept of formal ownership. As time passed, these people aged, but with an
increasing population, new fields would have had to be planted to increase production
to meet the needs of a growing population. Younger farmers could have established
these new fields further away, which would be less of a problem with respect to the
distance, as youth and strength would minimise the extra distance.
When land suddenly became available (after a death of the person farming it for
example), it is conceivable that a parent teaching their children their livelihood (in
this case farming) would have resulted in the child developing a certain affinity to
the land, leaving them with a strong desire to continue using that land. This, if it
happened, would put a younger person onto land that is closer (and possibly more
desirable for being so) to the settlement. For each subsequent generation where this
occurs, the connection to that land would become stronger, and concepts of ownership
and prestige could begin to take shape5.8 .

about what could have resulted from this growth.

Potential Problematic Scenario

There are problems that could have arisen from increasing reliance on crops. Animals
are food energy stores; they can act as a buffer to offset lean time and if necessary,
the breeding herd itself can be eaten if food shortages occur. Produce from crops can
be stored, but this requires surplus production at some point. If for example, drought
conditions persisted beyond the capacity of stored surplus there would not be as many
animals to ‘tide them over’ until the drought ended. Animals can be taken to regions
where the rain falls; fields of crops cannot be. If, for the first time, large communities had switched their food production strategy to rely heavily on crops, there would
have been no precedent for dealing with sustained crop failures, which thus could have
proven disastrous. If food shortages did occur on a regional level, and everyone had
switched to this new crop based economy, there would have been opportunity for conflict. People would have had no choice but to try to increase their herds, and graze
them where grass was available.
There are potential problems with this. Problems would have arisen from competition for access to grazing land which did get the rains and had grass available for grazing. Under these conditions, disputes would certainly have escalated in both frequency
and seriousness during times of food shortages, and relationships between communities and their inhabitants would have been strained. If food shortages from drought
(in an economy heavily reliant on crop production) occurred, it would be only natural for people to try to secure their animals, which would have become very valuable
commodities.
One problem with reverting back to animals as a food source in a sustained period
of food shortage is that of supply; more animals need to be eaten, but to sustain this the
size of the herds must be increased. It takes time to increase herd sizes without external
additions, as animals need to reach sexual maturity before they can reproduce. This is
problematic because if there was sustained drought that caused food shortages, there
would suddenly have been an increased call for meat to offset these food shortages, so
the animals which would have been used to increase the size of the herd would have
to be eaten. A possible solution to this would be to get more animals from wherever
they could, and this could include taking them from smaller, weaker settlements. It
would have been much easier to take animals than it would have been to take stores
of grain; to take these would have meant raiding the more dense ‘core’ of a community’s land or even needing to invade their settlement itself to get their grain stores.
Taking herds of animals from shepherds far from their homes would have been much
easier. Obviously, this would cause political upheaval and relationships between communities would change. It is easy to imagine communities responding to this by trying
to increase the security of their herds and food stores. After one experience like that
mentioned above, it would be understandable for communities to have, once the food
325

shortage crisis was over, have done things like build walls around their settlements,
and forge allegiances with those communities with which they still had good relations.
Scenarios like this could have been the impetus for the walled communities we see
appearing in the EB II in the southern Levant.
If a settlement had to contend with a growing population, but its total access to land
was limited, changing strategy could accommodate this growth. The change would
appear to be decreased meat reliance, increased dairy utilisation, and foddering of animals (primarily the cattle, which could be used for traction, increasing agricultural
output). Although these strategies have been shown to increase the amount of cropland, sometimes dramatically, the overall footprint is least affected. If a community
had been able to start using land that had previously been used as grazing land for
crop production, larger populations could be supported on the same amount of land. A
change like this would have resulted in a much heavier impact on the landscape as the
land which had previously been used for grazing became intensively utilised for crop
production.
Issues at play here are those imposed by the sheer scale of the catchment. As seen
in many of the catchments for large populations, the entire NE corner of the valley
was required to sustain the community. Expansion of cropland beyond the natural
boundaries of the rivers could have been problematic, especially if neighbouring contemporary sites were using the land for their own needs. If regionally increasing populations were accommodated by simply scaling up production under the same strategies,
problems could appear. This appears to have been the case at Shuna; the increasing
population seen from the Chalcolithic to the EEBI shows little evidence of a change in
strategy with the large number of pigs indicating a high level of reliance on meat. If
this was the general trend regionally, competition for grazing lands could have forced
new approaches. The fact that there was a drastic decline in pigs following the population growth in the EEBI at Shuna suggests that a change in strategy did indeed occur.
The models presented above suggest that this change could have been triggered, in
part, by limited access to grazing land and in part by a problem of scale. Extents of
grazing lands for larger populations were long distances from the settlement. This is
important, because having to graze their herds far away from ‘home’ would have meant
that shepherds and their herds would not have had the security given from proximity
to their community and it’s more densely utilised croplands which would have been a
picture of activity throughout most of the year.

past land use practices would have operated. This means that the model indicates the
